Quadratics can be defined as a polynomial equation of a second degree, which implies that it comprises a minimum of one term that is squared. It is also called quadratic equations. The general form of the quadratic equation is:

\(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)

where \(x\) is an unknown variable and \(a, b, c\) are numerical coefficients.

For example, \(x^2 + 2x + 1\) is a quadratic or quadratic equation. Here, \(a \neq 0\) because if it equals zero then the equation will not remain quadratic anymore and it will become a linear equation, such as:

\(bx + c = 0\)

Thus, this equation cannot be called a quadratic equation.

The terms \(a, b\) and \(c\) are also called quadratic coefficients.

What is Quadratic Equation?

The polynomial equation whose highest degree is two is called a quadratic equation or sometimes just quadratics. It is expressed in the form of:

\(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)

where \(x\) is the unknown variable and \(a, b\) and \(c\) are the constant terms.

Quadratics can be defined as a polynomial equation of a second degree, which implies that it comprises a minimum of one term that is squared. It is also called quadratic equations. The general form of the quadratic equation is:

\(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)

Quadratics Formula

The formula for a quadratic equation is used to find the roots of the equation. Since quadratics have a degree equal to two, therefore there will be two solutions for the equation. Suppose \(ax² + bx + c = 0\) is the quadratic equation, then the formula to find the roots of this equation will be:

\(x = \frac{-b\ \pm \ \sqrt{b² - 4ac}}{2a}\)

The sign of plus/minus indicates there will be two solutions for \(x\).

Examples of Quadratics

Following are the illustrations of quadratic equations of the form (ax² + bx + c = 0):

  • \(x^2 + x – 9 = 0\)
  • \(6x^2 – 2x – 6 = 9\)
  • \(8x^2 + 4x + 8 = 0\)
  • \(-x^2 + 5x + 12 = 0\)
Examples of a quadratic equation with the absence of a ‘C’- a constant term:
  • \(-x^2 – 9x = 0\)
  • \(x^2 + 2x = 0\)
  • \(-6x^2 – 3x = 0\)
  • \(-5x^2 + x = 0\)
  • \(-12x^2 + 13x = 0\)
  • \(11x^2 – 27x = 0\)
Following are the examples of a quadratic equation in factored form:
  • \((x – 6)(x + 1) = 0\)
  • \(-3(x – 4)(2x + 3) = 0\)
  • \((x − 5)(x + 3) = 0\)
  • \((x – 5)(x + 2) = 0\)
  • \((x – 4)(x + 2) = 0\)
  • \((2x + 3)(3x – 2) = 0\)
Below are the examples of a quadratic equation with an absence of linear co – efficient ‘bx’:
  • \(2x^2 – 64 = 0\)
  • \(x^2 – 16 = 0\)
  • \(9x^2 + 49 = 0\)
  • \(-2x^2 – 4 = 0\)
  • \(4x^2 + 81 = 0\)
  • \(-x^2 – 9 = 0\)

How to Solve Quadratic Equations?

There are basically four methods of solving quadratic equations. They are:

  • Factoring
  • Completing the square
  • Using Quadratic Formula
  • Taking the square root

Factoring of Quadratics

To factorize a quadratic equation, follow these steps:

Begin with an equation of the form \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\).
Ensure that it is set to zero.
Factor the left-hand side of the equation by assuming zero on the right-hand side of the equation.
Assign each factor equal to zero.
Now solve the equation to determine the values of \(x\).
If the leading coefficient (\(a\)) is not equal to one, follow a specific method for factoring.

Example:

\(2x^2 - x - 6 = 0\)
\((2x + 3)(x - 2) = 0\)
\(2x + 3 = 0\)
\(x = -\frac{3}{2}\)
\(x = 2\)

Completing the Square Method

Let's learn the Completing the Square method with an example.

Example: Solve \(2x^2 - x - 1 = 0\).
First, move the constant term to the other side of the equation.
\(2x^2 - x = 1\)
Dividing both sides by 2.
\(x^2 - \frac{x}{2} = \frac{1}{2}\)
Add the square of half of the coefficient of \(x\), \(\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2\), on both sides, i.e., \(\frac{1}{16}\)
\(x^2 - \frac{x}{2} + \frac{1}{16} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{16}\)
Now we can factor the right side,
\((x - \frac{1}{4})^2 = \frac{9}{16} = \left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^2\)
Taking the square root of both sides;
\(x - \frac{1}{4} = \pm\frac{3}{4}\)
Add \(\frac{1}{4}\) on both sides
\(x = \frac{1}{4} \pm \frac{3}{4}\)
Therefore,
\(x = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{4} = \frac{4}{4} = 1\)
\(x = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{3}{4} = -\frac{2}{4} = -\frac{1}{2}\)

Using Quadratic Formula

For the given Quadratic equation of the form, \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)

Therefore the roots of the given equation can be found by:

\(\frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\)

where \(\pm\) (one plus and one minus) represent two distinct roots of the given equation.

Taking the Square Root

We can use this method for the equations such as:

\(x^2 + a^2 = 0\)

Example: Solve \(x^2 - 50 = 0\).
\(x^2 - 50 = 0\)
\(x^2 = 50\)
Taking the square root of both sides
\(\sqrt{x^2} = \pm\sqrt{50}\)
\(x = \pm\sqrt{2 \times 5 \times 5}\)
\(x = \pm5\sqrt{2}\)

Thus, we got the required solution.
